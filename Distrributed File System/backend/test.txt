Garage sales can range from mildly entertaining to riduculous, depending upon your community.  But even if you live in a dull area full of dull people, I’ll give you some ideas to spice up the excitement of a garage sale.  On the other hand, if you happen to live in an eccentric community such as Santa Cruz, CA, then you have all of the ingredients you need to have an entertaining garage sale.

In the early summer of 2005, my girlfriend, Amber, and I were preparing to travel around Southeast Asia for an extended period.  One prerequisite of this journey was that we really had to slim down on our inventory of stuff because neither of us wanted to pay for a storage unit.  So we had a garage sale at our Santa Cruz house.  What started out as an exercise in necessity turned out to be a wildly entertaining experience.

The first customers showed up just after we got out of bed Saturday morning.  These early birds are the type that never able to wait until a decent hour to show up on your doorstep for a garage sale.  Maybe they are hoping to be the first ones in and score the gem.  Well, our first sale of the day was anything but a gem.  Amber had put out a half used container of Rolaids as a joke and to our surprise, this was our first sale: we got 25 cents for it.

Later that morning a lady showed up and introduced herself as Linda.  Apparently she was our neighbor, living in one of the large apartment complexes down the street.
Invoking our hospitality, Linda asked if she could sell some of her stuff at our garage sale.  Thinking this would be harmless, Amber and I consented.
Linda was gone in a flash and next thing we knew, she was back with a wreck of a pickup truck full of black plastic garbage bags.  She laid out a large, blue tarp in front of our garage and began spilling out the contents of those garbage bags onto the tarp.
Pretty soon, a mountain of worthless, abused clothing had formed in front of our garage.  Her heaping pile stood in stark contrast to the neat and orderly display of items Amber and I had arranged on tables covered in white butcher paper.  With Linda’s addition, Amber and I commented about the sudden degradation of our garage sale aesthetics.  I casually asked Linda “Hey, would you like a shovel or a rake to help spread out your stuff?”
Her work complete, Linda came into our garage to see our stuff for sale.  She noticed the CD player and stereo system and asked if we had ever heard of her favorite artist named something or another (I can’t remember who).  
We said “no”, so Linda ran home and grabbed a CD, promising we would love it.  Not bothering to ask permission, she popped the CD into my stereo system and cranked the volume of what turned out to be opera type music.
Linda fired up her vocal cords and began howling away.
Amber and I ran into the laundry room and slammed the door, almost dying of laughter.  We laughed so hard we ached; it was several minutes before we composed ourselves.  Once composed, we reentered the garage to inspect for collateral damage- we feared that Linda would have scared away all other customers.

I decided the only way to delude myself into believing there was a real danger was to watch the news or perhaps a cheesy horror movie.  Rather than fall into a trap of fearing fear itself, I armed myself with knowledge.  I got to know George on the phone and decided that his personal story was either legitimate or the work of a highly sophisticated ax murderer.  In other words, he probably wasn’t making things up, but if he was then he was doing a really good job at it.  Also, I decided that I would use my camera phone to take photos of  him, his car and license plate and send these off at my earliest discretion to my girlfriend.  I figured these less than foolproof precautions constituted a reasonable insurance plan.

George turned out to be a morning person, evident in his requirement to pick me up at 6 am from my hotel room.  ( I was living out of a hotel room at the time, for that story click here ).  I noticed straight away that he was a bigger guy with lots of visible tattoos.  Uh-oh,  time to worry.  After all, it is a known fact that people with tattoos are as much as 0-100% more dangerous than people without tattoos.  Also, there were no license plates on the car, just auto dealership tags, so my camera-phone surveillance relay strategy was going to be challenged.  “Well heck”, I thought, as I loaded my backpack and a honeydew melon into the backseat.  At least he wasn’t kidding about moving.  The SUV was packed full of stuff.

Driving from the Bay Area to Tahoe, George and I got to know each other a little bit.  By the time we neared Donner Summit, George was getting hungry so he pulled off at a Burger King.  As he parked, he reached into the glovebox and pulled out a white paper bag containing a startling variety of pill bottles.

“What are those for?”, I asked.

“This is my anti-psychotic medication”, he replied, then laughed.

“No seriously”, George said, as he presented one pill bottle at a time.  “This one is for anti depression, this one a mood stabilizer, this one is for anxiety….”

Okay, I thought, so it’s not anti-psychotic medication, but I’m not exactly feeling comforted either.  “So what happens if you stop taking your medications, are you going to start getting weird on me?” I asked, not really joking.

“No, you’ve got nothing to worry about”, George reassured, “I’ll tell you about it later”.

After a nutritional attack of burgers and french fries, we continued our drive.  I guess George was beginning to trust me because he began to tell me about his past, his childhood, and the reasons for all those medications.  For the sake of George’s privacy, I won’t repeat that conversation, but suffice it to say that he experienced a brutal childhood and some rough breaks later in life including a sudden and unexpected divorce, and had only recently begun to get therapy for these traumas.  I began to have sympathy for the guy and realized that he was just like everybody else; a guy trying to do the best he can with the cards he has been dealt.

Driving with George turned out to not only be enjoyable but also efficient.  George worked as a truck driver, and he knew how to really peel off the miles.  Furthermore, we were caravaning with a friend of George named George (a.k.a. Jimmy to avoid confusion) and his two teenagers.  I didn’t realize this until we were already on the road, but it was comforting to know we were caravaning with a guy who had kids.  Any worries that I had entertained about George were swept away and I began to really enjoy the journey.  George and I swapped stories back and forth, mile after mile.  It turned out that George had some of the same concerns about me as I had about him, prior to us meeting.  I have a very deep voice, and after talking with each other on the phone, George had envisioned that I would be a very big guy (I am not).  In fact, George said “I was wondering if I would even have enough room in my car for you.”  We both laughed at this assumption because to meet me in person is to meet someone who is almost certainly smaller.

By the time we hit Nevada I was reading George’s text messages to him and writing replies (on his cell phone).  One text message from his sister read “where are you now? How is the hitchhiker?”  I wrote back “Reno. Hitchhiker is great.”  We stopped that night in Pocatello, Idaho, and George and I split the cost of a hotel room.  Jimmy and his teenagers slept in the room next door.  I didn’t sleep too well; George turned out to be really good at snoring.  Next morning we hit the road and made excellent time, arriving in Bozeman at 11:15 am.  Those early morning starts made for good progress on the road, and before I knew it, our journey was over.  George had delivered outstanding door to door service and had been good company, plus a safe driver.  I wished him well, and without much ado he was off.

Getting back to the original intent of driving out to Montana with a stranger, the idea was to save money.  Did I accomplish this?  Between the hotel room (50% of $60) the gas, and the food, I figure I spent at least $170.  I was generous with my gas money contributions to George because let’s face it; he provided door to door service and assumed all of the wear and tear on his vehicle, plus he did all of the driving.  The absolute cheapest airfare I could find was $219-$325 each way, depending upon the date chosen (round trip airfares were equally or more expensive).  So I did save some money, not a huge amount, but I got an experience too.  Nine days later, after a great time in Bozeman, I flew home.  I thought it was kind of strange how flying took me so quickly from one place to another, there was no real transition.  The driving experience was more impressive, it provided a sense of just how big and diverse this country is.    I realized that our society has sacrificed certain aspects of the life experience for the sake of speed and the notion that time is money.  While speed and timeliness are important aspects of our contemporary lifestyles, sometimes we lose a bit of what is good in life by being hectic.  Sometimes it is good to go slowly.
